# Fraud Clarity Review & Reliance Router Skill

**Version:** 2026-07-07  
**Primary use:** Review Fraud case records for fraud clarity, reliance, semantic conversion, authorization, accounting, damages, and missing bridge records.  
**Core audience:** Counsel, investigator, forensic accountant, or case reviewer who does not already understand the deal.

---

## 1. Mission

This skill converts scattered records into a legally useful, plain-English fraud-indicator analysis. It is not enough to identify a document or quote a figure. Every relevant record must be tested for whether it helps explain:

1. what Bryce was told, shown, asked to sign, or led to believe;
2. what Bryce understood at the time;
3. what later changed;
4. what label or number changed meaning;
5. what money, credit, authorization, debt, lien risk, or litigation exposure shifted onto Bryce;
6. who benefited from the changed meaning;
7. what fraud, misrepresentation, omission, reliance, causation, damages, or bridge-record issue counsel should evaluate.

The reviewer must write for a new reader. Do not assume the reader already understands the real-estate transaction, the construction figures, the loan mechanics, the draw process, or the difference between a contract price, a budget, an estimate, a draw, an owner payment, a credit, and a claimed balance.

---

## 2. When to use this skill

Use this skill whenever the task involves any of the following:

- fraud or misrepresentation review;
- RICO / enterprise-pattern fact routing;
- real-estate purchase or construction cost dispute;
- “budget” versus “contract” comparison;
- owner-paid item classification;
- draw request authorization;
- disputed reimbursement or credit treatment;
- change-order / amendment / approval gaps;
- reliance, inducement, or decision-pivot analysis;
- explaining why Bryce chose the Jeff / Colby / Suited transaction instead of the home he was already buying;
- preparing counsel packets, evidence maps, deposition outlines, demand drafts, or discovery plans.

## 3. Non-negotiable review rules

### 3.1 Do not summarize only

A document summary is not enough. For each record, answer:

> Does this help show or explain fraud indicators, reliance, semantic conversion, missing authorization, money application, damages, or a missing bridge record?

### 3.2 Decode every figure

Never drop a number into the draft and assume the reader understands it. For every major number, explain:

- what the figure is;
- what it is not;
- where it came from;
- how it was later used;
- whether the label changed;
- the delta from the baseline;
- who benefited from the changed meaning;
- what bridge record would make the later use legitimate.

### 3.3 Separate the money buckets

Always separate:

- land price;
- construction price;
- total budget;
- loan amount;
- draw amount;
- owner payment;
- vendor invoice;
- owner credit;
- claimed balance;
- lien / title / payoff risk.

### 3.4 Use professional review language

Use:

- “The record response is…”
- “Counsel should evaluate…”
- “The bridge record needed is…”
- “The current record creates a priority discovery issue…”
- “The issue is not merely cost overrun; it is whether the signed baseline and Bryce’s reliance were later recharacterized without an authenticated owner-approved bridge.”

Do not weaken the analysis with unnecessary disclaimers such as “this does not prove fraud.” The correct framing is:

> “This record supports a fraud-indicator / reliance / authorization issue for counsel to evaluate.”

---

## 4. Source tiering

Classify every fact by proof tier.

| Tier | Label | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| F0 | Native source | Original or native record: signed contract, email, text, bank record, draw record, title record, invoice, payment proof, metadata-bearing file. |
| F1 | Extracted source | OCR, transcript, parsed export, spreadsheet extract, summary export, Bates text, or converted copy. |
| F2 | Analysis | Calculations, timelines, semantic mapping, inference, cross-document comparison. |
| Open | Bridge gap | A necessary proof link that has not yet been located. |

Do not convert an F2 inference into an F0 fact. Keep the source tier visible.

## 6. Fraud-element test

For each fact, document, number, communication, payment, or omission, test the following elements.

| Issue | Search must answer | Plain-English explanation required |
|---|---|---|
| Representation | What was Bryce told, shown, asked to sign, or led to believe? | “This is the statement or document that shaped Bryce’s understanding.” |
| Omission / nondisclosure | What material fact was not disclosed at the time? | “This matters because Bryce could not evaluate the deal without knowing this.” |
| Falsity / inconsistency | What later record conflicts with the earlier representation? | “The same number or label later changed meaning.” |
| Materiality | Would the fact affect a buyer/borrower’s decision? | “This was not a minor detail; it affected price, debt, risk, authorization, or whether Bryce would proceed.” |
| Reliance | Did Bryce act because of the representation? | “This explains why Bryce chose this path instead of the other home.” |
| Causation | What did the representation cause Bryce to do? | “Bryce signed, closed, funded, paid, or stayed in the project because of this understanding.” |
| Damages / injury | What money, credit, debt, lien risk, or claim followed? | “This is the financial consequence of the changed meaning.” |
| Bridge record | What record should exist if the adverse story is true? | “If they were right, there should be a signed amendment, change order, owner approval, vendor proof, payment proof, or payment-credit reconciliation.” |

## 7. Figure-clarity protocol

Use this structure for every major number:

```text
Figure:
[$ amount]

What it is:
[Construction price, land price, total budget, loan amount, owner payment, draw request, claimed balance, etc.]

What it is not:
[Not land / not construction / not owner authorization / not final cost / not proof of debt.]

Source:
[Document, date, Bates/file ID.]

Why it matters:
[Legal/accounting/reliance consequence.]

What changed later:
[Later figure, label, or claim.]

Delta:
[Difference, calculated plainly.]

Fraud-clarity point:
[Misrepresentation, nondisclosure, semantic conversion, missing authorization, payment-credit issue, or damages.]

Bridge record needed:
[Signed change order, amendment, owner approval, native workbook, vendor proof, draw proof, payment proof, credit reconciliation.]
```

### Default construction-baseline example

```text
Figure:
$791,456

What it is:
The signed construction baseline excluding the $235,000 lot.

What it is not:
It is not merely an informal estimate unless a later authenticated owner-approved bridge displaced it.

What changed later:
The later construction figure became $1,104,826, derived from the $1,339,826 Build Job Budget less the $235,000 land component.

Delta:
$313,370 above the signed construction baseline.

Fraud-clarity point:
The issue is whether a signed construction price was later recharacterized through labels such as “estimate,” “budget,” “overage,” or “nicer home” without the required owner-approved bridge.

Bridge record needed:
Signed change order, signed amendment, native workbook transmittal, owner-facing acceptance, vendor proof, proof of payment, and credit reconciliation.
```

## 8. Bryce decision-pivot module

The packet must contain a dedicated section titled:

> **Why I Chose This Transaction Instead of the Home I Was Already Buying**

Do not treat this as a side note. The reader must understand the threshold reliance decision.

```text

- Contract status:
- Timeline:
- What Bryce expected from that deal:

- Lot/home:
- Claimed value:
- Claimed cost:

- Financing representation:
- Investment or upside representation:

- Lower apparent cost?
- Better home?
- Investment upside?
- Trusted builder/realtor relationship?
- Reimbursement expectation?
- Controlled construction budget?
- Appraisal/loan support?

- Signed REPC?
- Cost breakdown?
- Colby communications?
- Jeff/Suited statements?
- Appraisal or lender materials?
- Draw reimbursement representations?
- Source records needed:

5. What did Bryce give up or expose himself to by pivoting?
- Original home opportunity:
- New loan obligation:
- Cash to close:
- Owner payments:
- Draw risk:
- Claimed balance:
- Litigation exposure:
- Source records needed:

6. Who benefited from Bryce entering this transaction?
- Builder benefit:
- Agent/broker benefit:
- Lender/loan benefit:
- Project funding benefit:
- Any front-borrower / reliance issue:
- Source records needed:
```

### Plain-English reliance paragraph template

```text
Before the disputed draws and final accounting issues, I faced a threshold decision: whether to continue with the home I was already buying or move into the Jeff Strong / Suited Construction / Colby Peterson transaction. That decision matters because reliance does not begin at the final accounting stage. It begins with the representations that caused me to change course.

The records should be reviewed to determine what I was told about price, budget, scope, financing, reimbursement, risk, and value at the time I made that decision. If the later position is that the signed construction number was only an estimate, that owner-paid items were my separate debt, or that the project was materially more expensive than represented, counsel should evaluate whether those facts were disclosed before I gave up the other path and committed to this one.

The question is not merely whether the house ultimately cost more. The question is whether I was induced into the transaction by a set of representations that later changed meaning after I was already committed.
```

## 9. Decision-pivot search prompt

Use this exact search prompt when searching the file set:

```text
Search for records explaining why Bryce Jones decided to buy or build the Jeff Strong / Suited Construction / Colby Peterson home instead of the home he was already buying.

Prioritize:
1. communications before the pivot;
2. investment-pitch records;
3. Colby Peterson communications;
4. Jeff Strong / Suited representations;
5. REPC and cost breakdown records;
6. appraisal / loan / value representations;
7. records showing what Bryce understood about cost, scope, reimbursement, and risk;
8. records showing the original home Bryce was buying;
9. records showing what Bryce gave up or changed by moving into the Suited transaction;
10. records showing who benefited from Bryce becoming the buyer/borrower.

For each result, extract:
- date;
- speaker;
- recipient;
- exact statement or figure;
- audience;
- what Bryce likely understood;
- later inconsistent use;
- fraud/reliance element implicated;
- source tier;
- bridge record needed.
```

## 11. Builder / agent claim response script

Use this when testing opposing explanations.

```text
Opposing claim:
[Example: “Bryce wanted upgrades and a nicer home.”]

Record response:
[State the strongest source-based answer.]

Why this matters:
[Explain why the claim changes legal/accounting meaning.]

Fraud/reliance issue:
[Misrepresentation / omission / semantic conversion / unauthorized cost shift / credit issue.]

What the claim would need:
[Signed change order, owner-facing approval, native budget, vendor proof, credit ledger, etc.]

Counsel question:
[Ask what legal effect follows if the bridge record does not exist.]
```

### Example

```text
Opposing claim:
Bryce chose upgrades and a nicer home.

Record response:
Specific owner choices must be separated from global cost inflation. A preference for a nice home is not a blank authorization to move the construction obligation from $791,456 to $1,104,826 without itemized change authorization, vendor support, payment proof, and credit treatment.

Why this matters:
The adverse explanation attempts to convert general preference into financial authorization.

Fraud/reliance issue:
Semantic conversion, material omission, authorization, damages, and owner-credit treatment.

What the claim would need:
Itemized change orders, owner-facing approvals, invoices, proof of payment, and a credit schedule.

Counsel question:
Can the builder enforce the higher figure if the increase is not tied to authenticated owner-approved changes?
```

## 12. Review checklist

Before finalizing any section, confirm:

```text
1. Did I identify the specific statement, number, document, or omission?
2. Did I explain who said it, who received it, and when?
3. Did I explain what Bryce understood at the time?
4. Did I explain how the meaning later changed?
5. Did I calculate the dollar difference in plain English?
6. Did I separate land, construction, loan funds, owner payments, credits, and claimed debt?
7. Did I identify who benefited from the changed meaning?
8. Did I connect the fact to reliance, authorization, credit, damages, or nondisclosure?
9. Did I avoid conclusory language where counsel needs to evaluate?
10. Did I avoid weakening language like “this does not prove fraud”?
11. Did I state the bridge record that should exist if the other side’s version is true?
12. Did I answer why this mattered to Bryce before he committed?
```

If any answer is “no,” the section is not ready.

- the representation;
